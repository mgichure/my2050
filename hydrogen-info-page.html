This lever allows you to choose how much of our natural gas grid is converted to supply hydrogen gas by setting an ambition level for hydrogen by 2050, ranging from level 1 (minimum effort) to level 4 (maximum effort).

Natural gas is widely used for heating and cooking in 85% of UK homes, as well as in industrial processes. However, its combustion releases carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions. Transitioning the gas grid to hydrogen could eliminate emissions, as hydrogen combustion does not release CO2. Bulk hydrogen production already exists through steam methane reforming (SMR) of natural gas. To reduce greenhouse gas emissions, low carbon methods are needed, such as capturing and storing CO2 from SMR (‘blue’ hydrogen) or producing hydrogen through electrolysis using low carbon electricity (‘green’ hydrogen). Hydrogen leakage has a negligible effect on climate as it is not considered a greenhouse gas.

Interaction with other levers

Low-carbon hydrogen can be produced in a number of ways, such as from biomass, from natural gas with carbon capture and storage (CCS), or from electrolysis of water. Electrolysis requires electricity, so enough low-carbon electricity needs to be generated by the Electricity levers for nuclear and renewables. If the Carbon Capture lever is deployed, it allows hydrogen produced by steam methane reformation of natural gas to be decarbonising. If there is enough biomass supply from the Bioenergy lever, then biomass can be combined with carbon capture to generate negative CO2 emissions.
